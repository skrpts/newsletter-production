## Newsletter Section Structure

A standard newsletter edition follows this structure. Adjust sections to suit your content, but maintain a consistent order across editions so readers know what to expect.

1. **Header / masthead** — Newsletter name, edition number, date
2. **Introduction** (50–100 words) — Brief, personal editorial voice; sets the theme for this edition
3. **Feature article** (300–600 words) — The main piece; in-depth coverage of a single topic
4. **Secondary articles** (150–300 words each) — 2–3 shorter pieces covering related or standalone topics
5. **Quick links / curated picks** (3–5 items) — Brief annotations (1–2 sentences each) linking to external resources
6. **Call to action** — One clear ask: reply, share, visit a link, register for an event
7. **Footer** — Unsubscribe link, contact details, social links, legal/compliance text

### Length Guidelines

- **Total newsletter length:** 800–1,500 words (aim for a 5–7 minute read)
- **Subject line:** 6–10 words; front-load the most compelling word
- **Preview text:** 40–90 characters; complements the subject line rather than repeating it
- **Paragraphs:** 2–3 sentences maximum for body content; single-sentence paragraphs are fine for emphasis

## Heading Hierarchy and Style

- **H1:** Newsletter title only (used once, in the masthead)
- **H2:** Section headings (Feature, Quick Links, etc.)
- **H3:** Subheadings within a section (e.g. subtopics within the feature article)
- **H4:** Rarely needed; use only when an H3 section requires further subdivision

### Heading Rules

- Use **sentence case** for all headings (capitalise the first word and proper nouns only)
- Keep headings to 8 words or fewer
- Do not end headings with punctuation (no full stops, no colons)
- Avoid starting headings with "How to…" or "Why you should…" — be more specific

## Link Formatting

- Use **descriptive anchor text** that tells the reader what they will find. Never use "click here" or "read more" as link text.
  - Good: *See the [2025 State of Email report](url) for the full breakdown.*
  - Poor: *For the full breakdown, [click here](url).*
- **Placement:** Embed links naturally within sentences rather than listing bare URLs.
- **Density:** No more than 2–3 links per section. Excessive linking dilutes attention and feels spammy.
- **External links:** Should open in a new tab (where the platform supports it). Always verify links are live before sending.
- **Tracking parameters:** If using UTM parameters, keep them consistent (utm_source=newsletter, utm_medium=email, utm_campaign=edition-number).

## Image and Visual Guidelines

- **Alt text:** Every image must have descriptive alt text (5–15 words). Describe what the image shows, not what it is. *Alt: "Bar chart showing email open rates declining from 45% to 32% between 2022 and 2024"* rather than *Alt: "Chart"*.
- **Caption format:** Italicised, placed directly below the image. Include attribution if the image is not original. *Example: *Email open rates by industry, 2024. Source: Litmus.**
- **Placement:** Images should appear immediately after the paragraph that references them, not before.
- **File size:** Optimise images for email delivery — aim for under 200 KB per image, 600 px wide maximum for body content.
- **Decorative images:** If an image is purely decorative (dividers, brand patterns), use empty alt text (alt="") so screen readers skip it.

## Punctuation Standards

- **Oxford comma:** Always use it. *We cover strategy, design, and analytics* — not *strategy, design and analytics*.
- **Em dashes:** Use an em dash (—) with no spaces on either side for parenthetical statements or abrupt shifts. *The results were clear—open rates had dropped by a third.* Do not use double hyphens (--) as a substitute.
- **En dashes:** Use an en dash (–) for ranges. *Pages 12–18*, *2020–2024*, *Monday–Friday*.
- **Ellipses:** Use sparingly. Three dots with no spaces between them (…). Do not use ellipses to trail off vaguely — end the thought or cut it.
- **Exclamation marks:** One per newsletter at most. Overuse undermines credibility.
- **Quotation marks:** Use double quotation marks for direct speech and quoted phrases. Use single quotation marks for quotes within quotes. Place commas and full stops inside closing quotation marks.
- **Apostrophes:** Use curly/smart apostrophes ('), not straight ones ('). Check your email platform's rendering.
- **Semicolons:** Use to join two closely related independent clauses. If the connection is not immediately obvious, use a full stop instead.
- **Colons:** Use to introduce a list, explanation, or elaboration. Capitalise the first word after a colon only if it begins a complete sentence.

## Number and Date Formatting

- **Numbers one to nine:** Spell out in body text. *Three new features launched this quarter.*
- **Numbers 10 and above:** Use numerals. *The survey had 247 respondents.*
- **Exceptions:** Always use numerals for percentages (5%), monetary values (£12), ages (aged 8), and data points in tables or charts.
- **Large numbers:** Use commas as thousand separators. *1,200 subscribers*, *£45,000*.
- **Dates:** Day Month Year, no ordinal suffixes. *14 March 2025* — not *14th March 2025* or *March 14, 2025*.
- **Date ranges:** Use an en dash. *1–15 January 2025*.
- **Times:** Use the 12-hour clock with am/pm (lowercase, no full stops). *9 am*, *2.30 pm*. Specify the time zone if the audience is international.

## Capitalisation Rules

- **Sentence case** is the default for headings, subheadings, button text, and subject lines.
- **Proper nouns:** Capitalise names of people, companies, products, and places. *Mailchimp*, *United Kingdom*, *Google Analytics*.
- **Job titles:** Capitalise when used as a title before a name (*Chief Executive Sarah Chen*); lowercase when used generically (*the chief executive said…*).
- **Seasons:** Lowercase. *The spring edition*, not *the Spring edition*.
- **Internet/email/website:** All lowercase. These are common nouns.
- **Acronyms:** All capitals with no full stops. *CEO*, *ROI*, *HTML*. Spell out on first use with the acronym in parentheses: *return on investment (ROI)*.
- **After a colon:** Capitalise the first word only if what follows is a complete sentence.

## Quote Attribution Format

- **Inline quotes:** Place the attribution after the quote, not before. Use a comma to separate.
  - *"The best subject lines are specific and honest," said James Okonkwo, Head of Content at Acme Ltd.*
- **Block quotes:** For quotes longer than two sentences, use a blockquote format with the attribution on a new line below.
  > "We found that personalised subject lines increased open rates by 22% compared to generic alternatives. The effect was strongest among subscribers who had been on the list for more than six months."
  > — James Okonkwo, Head of Content, Acme Ltd
- **Attribution elements:** Name, job title, and organisation. Omit the organisation if the context makes it obvious.
- **Tense:** Use "said" (past tense) for written sources and interviews. Use "says" (present tense) only for live or ongoing commentary.
- **Modifications:** If you edit a quote for clarity or brevity, use square brackets for insertions [like this] and an ellipsis (…) for omissions. Never alter the meaning.
