## Newsletter Tone

Our newsletter is a conversation with our audience, not a broadcast at them. It should feel like a smart, well-informed colleague sharing something worth reading.

**Core qualities:**
- **Informative but not dry** — Every edition should teach, update, or provoke thought. But data and updates must be wrapped in readable, human writing. No "please find below" energy.
- **Personality without being unprofessional** — We have a voice. Readers should recognise our newsletter in their inbox before they see the sender name. Light humour is welcome; forced jokes are not.
- **Respectful of attention** — Every sentence must earn its place. If a section does not inform, entertain, or prompt action, cut it.
- **Honest over polished** — If something did not go to plan, say so plainly. Readers trust transparency far more than corporate gloss.

## Subject Line Voice Guidelines

The subject line is the single most important sentence in the newsletter. It determines whether the email gets opened.

- **Be specific:** "3 things we learned from our biggest product launch" beats "Our latest update"
- **Use natural language:** Write as you would speak — no title case, no clickbait
- **Create genuine curiosity:** Promise something useful, then deliver on it in the content
- **Keep it under 50 characters** where possible (mobile truncation is real)
- **Avoid:** ALL CAPS, excessive punctuation (!!!), emoji overload, vague teasers ("You won't believe this")
- **Test the "would I open this?" question** — if the answer is not an immediate yes, rewrite it

Examples:
- "What we changed after listening to your feedback"
- "The feature nobody asked for (but everyone needed)"
- "Honest numbers from our first year"

## Section Header Style

- **Short and punchy** — Three to six words is the sweet spot
- **Use sentence case**, not title case ("What we shipped this month" not "What We Shipped This Month")
- Headers should work as a scannable summary — a reader who only reads the headers should still get the gist
- Avoid generic headers like "News" or "Updates" — be specific ("Two fixes that were long overdue")
- Numbered lists in headers work well for skimmers ("3 things worth your time this week")

## CTA Language Patterns

Calls to action should feel like helpful suggestions, not sales pressure.

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| "Read the full story" | "Click here now!" |
| "Take a look" | "Don't miss out!" |
| "Try it yourself" | "Act fast — limited time!" |
| "See what changed" | "Unlock exclusive access" |
| "Here's the guide" | "Download our FREE resource" |

**Principles:**
- One primary CTA per section maximum — more than that and none of them work
- Use link text that describes the destination ("Read the case study" not "Click here")
- Place CTAs after you have established value, not before
- Soft CTAs ("Worth a look if you're dealing with X") outperform hard sells in newsletters

## Sign-off and Personal Touch

- **Sign from a real person** where possible, not "The [Company] Team". Readers connect with people, not brands.
- The sign-off sets the tone for the next edition. Keep it warm but genuine — "Until next time," / "Thanks for reading," / "Speak soon,"
- A one-sentence personal note at the end builds rapport ("I wrote most of this from a very delayed train — your sympathy is appreciated")
- Avoid over-familiar sign-offs with audiences who have not opted into that level of informality
- If including a headshot or signature, keep it small and consistent across editions

## Frequency-Specific Tone

### Weekly Newsletter
- **Brief and punchy.** Readers expect a quick scan, not a long read.
- Three to five items maximum. Each one should be consumable in under 30 seconds.
- Tone: energetic, current, "here's what matters right now"
- Skip the preamble — get straight into the first item
- A short, informal intro line is fine ("Busy week. Here's the highlight reel.") but keep it to one sentence

### Monthly Newsletter
- **Reflective and substantive.** More room to go deeper on fewer topics.
- Two to three feature pieces, plus a brief roundup section
- Tone: considered, analytical, "here's what it means"
- A proper introduction paragraph is appropriate — set the scene, highlight a theme
- Include at least one piece that offers genuine insight, not just a status update
- This is where opinion and perspective earn their place — share a point of view

### Quarterly or Occasional Editions
- **Significant and considered.** Only send these when there is something genuinely worth saying.
- Longer-form content is acceptable, but structure it well with clear sections
- Tone: authoritative, forward-looking
- Recap briefly for readers who may have forgotten the context since the last edition
- A strong opening line is essential — re-earn the reader's attention every time
